# December 2021: the source signature, narrowed

*Working analysis, 5 July 2026. Prompted by testing (and excluding) the heritage
railway as a candidate; not conclusions until the discriminating records are seen.*

## New evidence extracted from the hour-level record

1. **The chemistry excludes coal/steam.** During the 386 µg/m³ NO₂ hour
   (18 Dec 10:00), PM2.5 was **1.29 µg/m³** — effectively particle-free air. The
   whole plateau ran PM2.5 1.3–5. Steam-locomotive smoke is PM-dense; the Great
   Central Railway's December services (which were considered as a candidate,
   since the line passes 1–1.5 km west) are excluded for these events. Low PM
   also disfavours older, pre-filter diesels; it is consistent with modern
   (DPF-equipped) diesel engines, which emit little PM but abundant NOx.
2. **Each event day peaks at 09:00–10:00 with fresh NO** (51–55 µg/m³, NO/NO₂
   ≈ 0.1–0.4 — a source minutes upwind), then decays smoothly all day. No
   passage-spikes (excludes passing trains as the direct mechanism), no rush-hour
   double hump (excludes commuter traffic).
3. **No temperature dependence.** The coldest morning of the month (−0.6 °C,
   22 Dec) was not an exceedance day; the mildest (13.1 °C, 31 Dec) was. This
   excludes heating-demand-driven sources (boilers) and weather-only explanations.
4. **The regime is precisely bounded.** Quiet 1–15 Dec (except an isolated
   251 µg/m³ event on 8 Dec), continuously elevated **16–31 Dec 2021** (daily
   maxima 114–386, weekends and Christmas Day included), then *nothing* —
   not one day ≥100 in all of Jan–Mar 2022.

## The hypothesis that fits everything

**A Christmas-engineering ballast campaign at the Mountsorrel railhead.**
Network Rail's largest engineering window of the year ran 24 Dec 2021 – 4 Jan 2022
(£133M, >1,900 possessions), including ballasted-track renewal on the Midland Main
Line — whose on-line ballast source is Mountsorrel. A campaign of loading and
staging ballast trains (modern Class 66/70 traction: high NOx, low PM) in the two
weeks before and during the window fits:

- the bounded 16–31 Dec operating regime, ramping before the possession dates;
- daily operation regardless of weekends, holidays or temperature;
- morning (loading-shift) peaks with fresh-NO proximity;
- the deposited-dust trigger exceedances at Stn 9 in the same two monitoring
  periods, with directional indication SW/W (the rail-loading side) — ballast
  handling is dusty even when the exhaust is not;
- the only other exceedances in the record: **4 Jan 2023, 09:00–10:00** — squarely
  in the following year's New Year engineering window;
- the near-absence of similar Decembers since (engineering ballast demand varies
  by year and worksite location).

## Feb 2022 + Jan 2023 reports (obtained 5 Jul 2026): the dust component explained

The February 2022 compliance report attributes the record deposition event at
Stn 9 (223 mg/m²/day, "Very High", 28 Jan – 25 Feb 2022; directional Moderate
from the SW, "the direction of site") to **failing seals on the KVM screen
house** — "overly rigid material… allowing dust to escape" — replaced Feb–Mar
2022. The deposition arc across the NO₂-breach winter (188 → 127 → 223) tracks
this developing plant defect, which plausibly explains the *dust* signal of
Dec 2021–Feb 2022. It does **not** explain the NO₂: screening plant is
electrically driven. The combustion signal (morning-anchored, holiday-blind,
NO-rich, PM-free) remains attributed most plausibly to the rail/ballast
campaign, still awaiting dispatch records.

The January 2023 report confirms the 4 Jan 2023 exceedances passed through the
alerting system silently ("no trigger emails… were sent") — the alerts are
PM₁₀-based and blind to combustion NO₂ — and reports period winds overwhelmingly
from the **south** (source sector: stock area/sidings/estate side).

## Supporting evidence from later reports (5 Jul 2026)

- The December 2025 compliance report documents an Osiris alert (21 Nov 2025)
  caused by "**Loadout making washed ballast and material being sent to rail**"
  — direct confirmation that rail-ballast loading at the railhead is an
  alert-level emission source at this monitor, as the Dec 2021 hypothesis
  requires.
- The December 2022 report shows Christmas 2022 did *not* repeat the Dec 2021
  regime (no alerts, modest deposition, cold/dry month) — consistent with
  ballast campaigns varying year to year, and with the brief 4 Jan 2023 echo
  being the following season's only footprint.

## What would confirm or kill it

- **The quarry's rail dispatch records for Dec 2021 – Jan 2022 and Jan 2023** —
  already Request 2 in the council evidence report.
- Historic freight schedules from Mountsorrel Sidings (publicly searchable, e.g.
  Realtime Trains historic mode) for the same dates.
- Network Rail's East Midlands possession list for Christmas 2021.

## Note on posture

This hypothesis, if confirmed, frames December 2021 as a by-product of national
rail infrastructure logistics at the railhead — a scheduling/mitigation question
for the parties involved, not misconduct by anyone, and nothing to do with the
heritage railway. It strengthens the case for the existing, neutral asks (records
cross-reference; NO₂ visibility at site CM5) rather than adding new ones.
