# The 2026 NO₂ episodes are corridor events, strongest at Wolsey Way

*Working analysis, 5 July 2026. Source attribution in progress — this note records
evidence and hypotheses, not conclusions.*

## The observation

The 2026 NO₂ episodes are not single-station events at Hawcliffe Rd. Daily maxima
show **Wolsey Way (Loughborough) and Hawcliffe Rd (Mountsorrel) — 4 km apart along
the railway corridor — rising and falling together in multi-day blocks**, while
Ashby Rd, Whetstone Way and Cobden School stay at ordinary levels (15–18 µg/m³)
throughout. Wolsey Way is consistently the stronger: its 23–24 June hours reached
**434 and 401 µg/m³** (Hawcliffe: 236, 206).

Two blocks so far in 2026 (daily max NO₂, µg/m³):

| Block | Days | Wolsey peak | Hawcliffe peak | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 21–30 May | 8 elevated days | 417 (Mon 25 May) | 220 | Overlaps the Flying Scotsman visit to GCR-Nottingham (16–31 May, return trips to Loughborough) — but 16–20 and 31 May are quiet, so the fit is partial |
| 22–26 June | 5 consecutive weekdays | 434 (Tue 23) | 236 | No public event identified; ends abruptly before the weekend |

The 12 Aug 2025 event (Hawcliffe 153, four-hour run) also fits the weekday-episodic
pattern.

## Signature details

- Episodes run ~11:00–22:00 UTC, sustained, on consecutive days — a *campaign*
  shape, not a rush-hour or single-event shape.
- Wolsey Way's chronic profile (2025–26 hours ≥40 µg/m³) is weekday-heavy and
  **Sunday-quiet (7.4% vs ~16% weekdays)** — the opposite of heritage-railway
  running patterns, which are Sunday-heavy. Normal GCR services do not visibly
  register.
- The Zephyr SO₂ channels co-rise with NO₂ during episodes at both stations, but
  the SO₂/NO₂ ratio is similar at both and electrochemical SO₂ cells cross-respond
  to NO₂ — treat the sulphur signal as unconfirmed.
- Geography: Wolsey Way sits ~200 m from the Great Central Railway locomotive shed;
  the corridor also carries the Mountsorrel quarry freight branch traffic, and
  **Brush Traction's Falcon Works** (locomotive works; load-bank testing runs
  diesel engines at high power for days during commissioning) is nearby in
  Loughborough. All three are candidate sources with different expected schedules.

## Hypotheses ranked by current fit

1. **Industrial campaign near Wolsey Way** (e.g. locomotive load-bank testing at
   Brush, or similar multi-day plant operation): fits the weekday blocks, the
   abrupt Friday stop in June, the Sunday-quiet chronic pattern, and the intensity
   gradient (Wolsey ≫ Hawcliffe). Does not yet explain why Hawcliffe co-moves 4 km
   south — wind transport on the right bearing could.
2. **Special heritage operations** (Flying Scotsman fortnight, galas — not normal
   services): fits the May block's timing loosely; does not fit the June weekday
   block or the quiet listed running days (31 May).
3. **Quarry rail loading campaigns**: fits weekday blocks and the corridor, less
   well the Wolsey-dominance.

## What would settle it

- GCR-Nottingham's actual running calendar for 16–31 May 2026 (which days trains
  ran to Loughborough).
- Any engineering possessions/track work on the corridor 22–26 June 2026.
- Whether Brush Traction conducted load-bank testing 22–26 June and 21–30 May 2026.
- Wind direction for the episode hours (DustScan monthly reports for May/June 2026,
  when issued — the quarry's weather station data would also test the
  transport-to-Hawcliffe question).

## Why it matters

- For the council report's Finding 3: Hawcliffe's 2026 exceedances are the southern
  edge of a corridor phenomenon whose epicentre is Wolsey Way, in Loughborough
  itself — where residents live closer to the peak concentrations (434 µg/m³ is
  more than double the hourly limit value).
- Wolsey Way's "exceptionally polluted one hour in six" (see the colourscale note)
  is not an abstraction: it has an identifiable episodic structure that records
  held by third parties could explain within a week of asking.
